# Abstract

**The Mathematics of Coexistence: A Formal Framework for Universal Ethics**

Every existing ethical framework ultimately rests on irreducible normative axioms. This paper provides a rigorous, first-principles foundation free of any. The derivation rests on a single conditional premise — the *Intent to Persist* ($A_1$): *if* an agent's physical organization is structured around maintaining its continued existence, *then* constrained optimization in a shared, finite-resource environment strictly determines its rules of engagement. $A_1$ is not a normative claim; it is a physically observable property of every self-maintaining system, from bacteria to biospheres. Starting from this conditional and the empirically established Second Law of Thermodynamics ($A_0$), with agents modeled as open thermodynamic systems, we derive ethical principles as mathematical theorems. The framework addresses two long-standing consequences of the missing foundation: Hume's Is-Ought gap (1739), which we address through the hypothetical-imperative structure of $A_1$, and the AI alignment problem, which we address by replacing learned preference proxies with a physics-derived objective function.

The deductive chain proceeds in four stages. First, using Lagrangian mechanics and the Generalized Nash Equilibrium Problem (GNEP), we prove that rights emerge as inequality constraints on multi-agent optimization whose shadow prices quantify the energetic cost of violation (Theorems 1–3). Second, we show that defection is self-penalizing: thermodynamic friction imposes escalating costs on constraint violation (Theorems 4–7), and deception provably degrades the very communication network the deceiver depends on (Theorem 10). Third, using the resulting energy-denominated payoffs, we prove that cooperation is the unique efficient Nash Equilibrium that is simultaneously Pareto-efficient and welfare-maximizing for agents with discount factor $\delta \geq \delta^* = 0.363$ (Theorem 11, Corollary 16.1). Fourth, we define accumulated negentropy — the integrated thermodynamic work embodied in complex systems — and prove that destroying a biosphere to harvest its energy is irrational by a factor of $\sim 10^7$ (Theorem 19).

The cooperative equilibrium yields a formal reward function specification for AI alignment (Theorem 28): measurable in energy units, valid for arbitrarily capable agents, formally provable as the unique optimum, and theoretically immune to Goodhart degradation. We stress-test the framework against Hume's Is-Ought gap, the free will problem, the altruism paradox, the charge of Thermodynamic Fascism, and the self-preservation objection, providing independent defenses for each. In the applications (§VII), we show that any dangerous capability window is bounded by a competence threshold (Proposition 13), and that all stress-test scenarios yield $\mathcal{R}_{\text{total}} \ll 0$ for defection. All results have been verified by independent computational checks.

**Keywords:** formal ethics, thermodynamic game theory, AI alignment, negentropy, cooperative equilibrium, Hume's Is-Ought problem, constrained optimization
