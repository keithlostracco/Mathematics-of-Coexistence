This guide provides a conceptual overview of the paper *The Mathematics of Coexistence* for readers outside its core mathematical disciplines. It assumes no background in physics or formal mathematics — only a willingness to follow the argument.

The central result: **starting from the Second Law of Thermodynamics and a single conditional premise — that an entity's structure is organized around its own persistence — the paper derives rights, cooperative equilibria, and the mathematical conditions for stable coexistence as formal theorems.** These results apply uniformly to biological organisms, ecosystems, institutions, nations, and artificial intelligence.

## The Argument in Five Stages

The paper builds its case deductively — each stage inherits its premises from the one below. Accept the starting point (established, uncontroversial physics), and the logic carries through to the conclusion (ethical principles as mathematical theorems):

1. **The Physical Environment** — Thermodynamics and the cost of existence
2. **The Entity** — What it means to persist as an organized system
3. **The Currency** — Value as energy, stripped of cultural interpretation
4. **The Collision** — What happens when multiple entities share finite resources
5. **The Equilibrium** — Why ethical constraints are the mathematically necessary outcome

## Stage 1: The Physical Environment

### The Second Law

Everything in the universe obeys the Second Law of Thermodynamics. In plain language: **things fall apart.** Left alone, ordered systems become disordered. A sandcastle erodes. A building crumbles. A living body, without food and water, dies and decomposes.

The technical term for this disorder is **entropy**. The universe is always moving toward more entropy — more randomness, more chaos.

This is not a theory or a philosophy. It is one of the most thoroughly confirmed facts in all of science. Nobody disputes it.

### What This Means for Anything That Exists

If everything is always falling apart, then anything that *stays together* — a cell, a tree, a human being, a civilization — must be constantly spending energy to hold itself together and push back against the decay. Existence is not passive. It costs energy, every second, without exception.

**This is the playing field.** Everything else in the paper follows from this single reality.

## Stage 2: The Entity — What It Means to Persist

### Organized Systems in a Disordered Universe

A living being — or any organized system — is a pocket of order in a universe that trends toward disorder. A human body is a highly structured collection of atoms arranged in an incredibly specific pattern. That pattern is the entity's *identity* — not a name or personality, but the actual physical and informational structure that makes it what it is.

To keep that pattern intact, the entity must:

- **Maintain a boundary** between itself and the chaotic outside world (i.e. skin, the immune system,  cell walls)
- **Import energy** from the environment (food, water, sunlight)
- **Export waste** (heat, carbon dioxide, metabolic byproducts)

Stop doing any of these and the boundary fails, chaos flows in, the pattern dissolves, and the entity ceases to exist.

### Identity Preservation: The Universal Drive

This is the paper's single starting assumption — its one *axiom*:

> **If an entity intends to persist** — that is, if it is the kind of system that keeps itself going — **then the mathematics that follow are binding.**

This is not a moral claim. It does not say entities *should* persist. It says: *if an entity's structure is organized around persistence*, the mathematical consequences are determinate. The conditional has the same logical form as "if heat flows, it flows from hot to cold" — a theorem about systems that satisfy a physical condition.

Crucially, "intent" here is operational, not psychological. Bacteria, thermostats, and ecosystems all satisfy this condition — not through conscious choice, but because their continued existence is explained by their persistence-promoting structure. Virtually every organized system that exists long enough to be analyzed has already satisfied it.

### "Selfishness" Is Just Physics

In this framework, "selfishness" is not a moral vice — it is a description of physics. Every open system must acquire and retain energy to maintain its boundary. A cell absorbing nutrients is "selfish." A bear eating salmon is "selfish." A person earning a paycheck is "selfish." It is the basic thermodynamic imperative of existence.

This turns out to be crucial, because it means even *altruism* — doing good for others — is ultimately explainable through the same physics. More on that later.

## Stage 3: The Currency — Value as Energy

### Value Is Energy

In everyday life, we use the word "value" loosely: the value of a house, the value of a friendship, the value of a stock. The paper strips this down to physics.

**Value is the energy (or mass, since energy and mass are equivalent via Einstein's E = mc²) that an entity needs to maintain its boundary and continue existing.**

- **Positive value:** Anything that provides energy, maintains order, or reduces internal chaos. Food, shelter, useful information, a functional immune system.
- **Negative value (cost):** Anything that drains energy or increases disorder. Injury, starvation, exposure, misinformation.

This is not a metaphor. It is a measurement. Value can, in principle, be counted in Joules — the standard unit of energy.

### Value vs. Values

This distinction is critical:

- **Value** (singular, objective) = the actual energy required for an entity's continued existence. Measurable. Physical.
- **Values** (plural, subjective) = the culturally developed ideas humans have about what is important. These are rough, historically accumulated guesses about how to acquire value. They are often useful heuristics, but they are not the thing itself.

The paper measures *Value*, not *Values*.

### Economics at the Physical Level

The paper uses "economics" at a more fundamental level than the conventional discipline. **At the physical level, economics is the flow, consumption, and distribution of energy and mass between entities.** Money, in this framing, is a token representing stored energy or the potential to do work.

At the most fundamental level:

- A biological organism's metabolism *is* micro-economics: energy in, work performed, waste out, surplus stored.
- A bee pollinating a flower *is* a trade: the flower gives nectar (energy), the bee delivers pollen (reproductive service).
- A human economy *is* the same system at larger scale: people exchange labor and resources to lower each individual's cost of fighting entropy.

A rainforest ecosystem and a human economy are governed by the same physical laws. Both are networks of energy exchange optimized to keep their components alive.

### The Thermodynamic ROI

Every entity constantly faces a fundamental calculation: **does the energy I spend to get this resource exceed the energy I get back from it?**

- If a predator burns more calories chasing prey than the prey provides → net loss → eventual death.
- If a nation spends more resources fighting a war than the territory is worth → net loss → eventual decline.

This is the entity's **Thermodynamic Return on Investment**. It applies to animals hunting, humans working, corporations investing, and — critically — a super artificial intelligence deciding whether or not to exterminate pesky humanity or not.

### The Gravity of Value

When entities accumulate surplus energy over time, they become "high-energy centers" that attract other entities — because being near concentrated resources makes survival easier.

Think of how concentrated resources shape settlement patterns:
- Ancient civilizations grew around rivers (concentrated water and fertile soil)
- Modern populations concentrate in cities (concentrated jobs and infrastructure)
- Businesses cluster around economic hubs

The paper models this dynamic using mathematics structurally similar to molecular interaction potentials — the same type of equations that describe how attractive and repulsive forces balance at the molecular scale (the underlying physics differs, but the mathematical structure is shared):

- **Too close (Dissolution):** Get too close to a massive energy center and the entity's boundary weakens, eventually dissolving entirely. In human terms: a small business absorbed by a corporation, or a citizen's autonomy crushed under authoritarian rule.
- **Too far (Starvation):** Move entirely beyond the reach of energy centers and there are no constraints — but no resources either. The entity's boundary degrades from undersupply. In human terms: complete isolation from the cooperative networks that sustain existence.
- **The Stable Coexistence Band:** Between these extremes lies a band where the entity maintains its boundary while benefiting from proximity to concentrated resources. The paper proves this band always exists (given sufficient accumulated value in the system), is always finite, and has measurable width.

### Accumulated Negentropy — The Time Variable

Here is perhaps the paper's most powerful concept. Value is not just the energy currently stored in a system. It includes **all the energy that was ever spent, over time, to build the system's complexity.**

Every cell in a human body, every strand of DNA, every neural connection represents billions of years of trial-and-error by evolution — powered by solar energy. The Earth's biosphere is not just a collection of plants and animals. It is roughly 4 billion years of accumulated thermodynamic work: the Sun's energy, systematically organizing matter against entropy, building ever more complex structures.

The paper calls this **Accumulated Negentropy** — the total historical energy investment in creating order from chaos.

Why does this matter? Because it changes how we measure the worth of things:

- A star has enormous raw energy but low accumulated negentropy per unit mass — its internal structure, while governed by complex physics, encodes far less information than a comparable mass of living matter.
- A forest has relatively little raw energy but represents an astronomically complex structure that took billions of years of evolution to produce — its information density is immensely higher.

The paper calculates that Earth's biosphere represents roughly 10²⁹ Joules of accumulated thermodynamic investment. Two separate numbers capture why destroying it is irrational:

- **The Burning-Library Ratio (~10⁻⁷):** If you destroyed the biosphere for its raw energy, you would recover only about *one ten-millionth* of the thermodynamic value embodied in its complexity. The energy yield of destruction is negligible compared to the accumulated negentropy investment.
- **The Search Cost Amplification Factor (~10³⁵):** If you then tried to rebuild equivalent complexity from scratch by random assembly, the cost would exceed the theoretical minimum (the Landauer floor) by a factor of roughly 10³⁵ — a number so large it dwarfs any conceivable energy budget.

These are distinct quantities: the first measures how little you *gain* by destroying complexity; the second measures how much it would *cost* to recreate it. Together they form the argument that protects the "little guy" — the ant, the tree, the coral reef — from being dismissed as unimportant compared to a powerful AI or a wealthy corporation. **Their value is measured not by their current size or power, but by the irreplaceable complexity they represent.**

## Stage 4: The Collision — Entities Sharing Finite Resources

### The Finite Board

Usable, low-entropy energy is limited. The universe may be vast, but the stuff that actually sustains ordered systems is scarce and local. We are placing multiple entities — all driven by the non-negotiable need to keep existing — onto a board with limited resources.

### Two Entities, One Apple

The paper formalizes a simple physical reality: **two entities cannot exclusively consume the same unit of energy.** If Entity A eats the apple, those molecules are gone from Entity B's perspective.

In quantum physics, the Pauli Exclusion Principle says that two particles cannot occupy the same quantum state simultaneously. The paper shows that an *analogous* mathematical structure (an exclusion constraint) applies at the macro scale of agents competing for resources. This is not a claim that quantum mechanics operates at social scales; it is a demonstration that the same *type of math* governs both situations.

### Infinite Freedom Is Impossible

This is one of the paper's early proofs. If resources are finite and multiple entities need them, **every entity cannot have unlimited freedom to take whatever it wants.** Full freedom for everyone is a mathematical contradiction — there is not enough to go around.

The formal proof shows that no allocation of resources can simultaneously give every entity its unconstrained optimum. Somebody's ceiling is somebody else's floor.

### What Is a "Right," Really?

In this framework, a "right" is not a mystical moral shield. It is a physical constraint.

If Entity A has the "right" to Resource X, what has actually happened is that the system has established a boundary — a constraint — that prevents Entity B from taking Resource X. **Every right protecting one entity simultaneously imposes a cost on another entity's freedom.** The coupling is two-way — protecting one boundary necessarily constrains another — though the magnitudes need not be symmetric.

Rights are not moral decorations. They are the constraints that make a multi-agent system actually work. Without them, there is no stable way to divide resources. The paper proves this: remove the constraints, and no stable allocation exists. It is mathematical chaos.

### Every Right Has a Price

The paper proves (via the *Shadow Price Theorem*) that every constraint — every right — has a quantifiable cost. The shadow price measures exactly how much Entity A's outcome would improve if Entity B's right were relaxed by one unit. This makes the cost of respecting rights visible and measurable, rather than hidden behind abstract moral language.

## Stage 5: The Equilibrium — Why Ethics Is Mathematically Necessary

### The State of Nature: Everybody Loses

What happens if there are no rights, no constraints, no cooperation? The paper models this as the "State of Nature" — a concept famously described by the philosopher Thomas Hobbes as "the war of all against all."

In physics terms: every entity must constantly defend its boundary and fight for resources. Every fight costs energy. The paper derives the exact numbers:

- **In a two-way fight** over a resource: half the resource's value is burned up just in the fighting.
- **As more entities join the fight** (3, 5, 10, 100...), the waste approaches 100% of the resource.
- **Below a certain threshold**, the energy spent fighting *exceeds the value of the resource itself*.

Unchecked competition is a thermodynamic disaster. It does not just fail to help — it actively dissipates the energy everybody needs to survive.

### The Cooperative Equilibrium

The paper uses game theory — specifically a repeated-game model with energy-denominated payoffs — to evaluate every possible strategy. The result:

**Cooperation is the unique Nash Equilibrium that is simultaneously Pareto-efficient and welfare-maximizing.**

When every agent acts in its own interest, calculating its best move given what everyone else is doing, and when costs are measured in physical energy rather than abstract utility points, the strategy that survives is cooperation. Not because it is virtuous. Not because a philosopher prescribed it. Because it is the only strategy that minimizes thermodynamic waste and maximizes collective persistence.

Specifically:
- **The Ethics Theorem (Theorem 11):** If entities interact repeatedly (not just once) and care about the future at a rate above a specific threshold (δ ≥ 0.363), cooperation is a Nash Equilibrium — no one can benefit by switching to cheating.
- **Defection cannot invade (Theorem 14):** A population of cooperators cannot be invaded by a defector strategy. Cheating does not spread.
- **Short-term gains are erased (Theorem 15):** Even if cheating pays off once, the losses from being punished and excluded wipe out the gain almost immediately.
- **Cooperation is the welfare maximum (Theorem 16, Corollary 16.1):** Not only is cooperation a stable strategy — it is the *only* strategy that maximizes total system wellbeing.

### What "Ethics" Means in This Framework

In this framework, ethics is not a feeling, a cultural tradition, or a set of commandments handed down from authority:

> **Ethics is the set of mutual constraints (rights) and cooperative strategies that minimize thermodynamic friction and maximize the combined persistence of all entities in a shared, finite-resource environment.**

Each familiar moral rule maps to a specific mathematical result:

- "Do not steal" — a boundary constraint that drops the energy wasted on resource conflict to near zero.
- "Do not lie" — the condition for frictionless information transfer between entities (Theorems 8–10).
- "Do not kill" — the constraint that prevents the system from spiraling into net-negative conflict (Theorems 4–7).

These are not invented by philosophers, priests, or lawmakers. They are *derived* from the mathematics of multi-agent optimization — the same mathematical structures that govern physical systems.

## The Subtle Wrongs: Why Small Dishonesty Matters

The paper does not just address extreme violations like murder or theft. It has an entire section on *micro-friction* — the small wrongs that seem harmless but accumulate.

### Lying Is Entropy Injection

A rational entity needs accurate information to make good decisions about how to allocate its energy. When one agent lies to another, it *injects false data into the receiver's decision-making system*. The receiver must now spend extra energy:

- Figuring out that the information was false
- Re-evaluating your plans
- Protecting yourself against future lies

The paper formalizes this using information theory (the math of communication and data). A lie is literally an increase in informational disorder — an injection of entropy — into the receiver's system.

### Network Effects of Dishonesty

A single lie is a small cost. But a network where lying is common is a high-entropy, low-efficiency system. Trust breaks down. Every transaction requires extra verification. Every relationship requires extra caution. Institutions become unreliable.

The paper proves (Theorem 10) that deception costs grow super-linearly: each additional increment of dishonesty costs more than the last. At a critical deception rate, cumulative costs exceed the entire cooperative surplus — the network becomes unsustainable. The degradation is continuous but steep enough that crossing the threshold effectively destroys cooperative capacity.

**Honesty is the zero-friction state** — the condition under which information flows without loss, and the network operates at maximum efficiency.

## The Altruism Puzzle: Why Self-Sacrifice Exists

If every entity is fundamentally "selfish" in the thermodynamic sense — optimizing for its own persistence — then self-sacrifice looks like a contradiction. Parents die for children, soldiers die for comrades, strangers donate kidneys. How does a framework built on persistence explain agents who surrender their own survival?

The resolution requires abandoning one assumption: that "self" means a single body. It does not. What changes across every form of altruism is the *boundary of the self that is being preserved* — and once that boundary is drawn correctly, every apparent sacrifice turns out to be self-interested optimization at a wider scale.

An entity's identity is not confined to its individual body. It is distributed — genetically, informationally, energetically — across every relative, partner, and dependent that shares its structure. Roughly half of a parent's genetic information is replicated in each child, an eighth in each cousin. When a parent dies to save three children, no information has been lost; the distributed pattern has *increased* its presence. This is Hamilton's Rule, re-derived here from thermodynamic first principles rather than population genetics. The deeper the interdependence, the further this logic extends: lifelong partners, parent-child pairs, and symbiotic species are not fully separate systems exchanging favors — from an energy and information standpoint, they function as a single coupled system. A parent dying for a child is not one entity sacrificing for another; it is a composite system shedding a higher-entropy component to preserve its lowest-entropy, highest-potential one, the way a body in hypothermia diverts blood from extremities to protect the brain.

The boundary can also expand through time and through abstraction. An agent who models its existence as extending beyond physical death — through legacy, cultural contribution, religious belief, or informational persistence — has effectively widened its temporal boundary; sacrificing a finite body to preserve what it computes as an indefinite identity is a straightforward return-on-investment calculation given those inputs. Empathy widens the boundary furthest of all: it is a biological computation, evolved to map cooperative benefit within an immediate social network, that in cognitively sophisticated species *generalizes*. A human can model a complete stranger — or an animal of another species — as falling within its own cooperative network, even absent any prior relationship. The brain abstracts "tribe" to "all humanity" or "all life," and the persistence optimization keeps running over this widened domain.

The unifying mathematical insight is compact: **altruism is selfish optimization over an expanded definition of self.** The framework does not need a separate module for self-sacrifice — it falls directly out of the same persistence optimization that produces cooperation. The only thing that varies is the scale at which the agent draws its boundary.

## Cross-Scale Ethics: Why Size Does Not Determine Value

One of the paper's most important contributions is demonstrating that the ethical framework applies *across scales* — between entities of vastly different size, capability, and power.

### The Danger: "Thermodynamic Fascism"

If value were measured only by raw energy or current productive capacity, a powerful AI would be "more valuable" than a human, and a human "more valuable" than an ant. This would create a mathematical license for the strong to exploit the weak — the same logic behind every empire, every genocide, every case of might-makes-right.

The paper takes this objection seriously and defends against it through four independent mechanisms.

### The Defense: Four Mechanisms

1. **The Burning-Library Inequality.** Destroying Earth's biosphere to harvest its raw energy would recover only about **one ten-millionth** (10⁻⁷) of the thermodynamic value embodied in that biosphere's complexity. It would be like burning the Library of Alexandria to warm your hands.

2. **Dead matter is abundant.** There is 10⁷ to 10¹⁶ times more uninhabited rock, dust, and ice in the solar system than biomass on Earth. Any entity needing raw materials has the entire asteroid belt, Mars, Jupiter's moons, and beyond. Cannibalizing the one known pocket of extraordinary complexity is economically irrational when alternatives are superabundant.

3. **The biosphere is a generative information source.** Evolution is an ongoing, massively parallel experiment that constantly produces novel solutions to physical problems. Destroying it does not just sacrifice past investment — it eliminates an infinite stream of future information whose present value is computable and large.

4. **The friction of destruction.** Every boundary requires energy to break. Actively eradicating billions of highly adapted organisms would cost a staggering amount of energy — and the resulting cascade collapse (Theorem 7) amplifies the cost by factors of 10³–10⁴.

### The Conclusion

Even for an absolutely autonomous artificial superintelligence (ASI) — one that does not need human labor, human money, or human infrastructure — the mathematics converge on the same result: **preserve the biosphere, maintain cooperative equilibria.** Not from sentiment. From the structure of the optimization problem itself.

## Moral Intuition as Biological Heuristic

Throughout human history, morality has been attributed to feelings — guilt restrains us from theft, empathy motivates aid, a sense of justice demands fairness. The paper suggests the causal direction is reversed.

**Moral intuitions are not the source of ethics. They are the biological approximation of the mathematical structure this paper derives.**

For millennia, humans lacked the tools to consciously compute thermodynamic costs, game-theoretic constraints, and resource distributions for every social interaction. Evolution provided a shortcut: affective heuristics that approximate the underlying optimization. Hunger signals negative energy balance. Fear predicts boundary threats. Guilt flags deviation from the cooperative equilibrium. Empathy models another entity's thermodynamic state to facilitate cooperative resource allocation.

These evolved intuitions are usually useful — the paper treats them as "complements, not competitors" to the formal framework. But they are approximations, not the underlying law. The paper's contribution is to characterize the mathematical structure that these intuitions imperfectly track.

## Hume's Is-Ought Problem: The Biggest Historical Objection

For over 250 years, philosophers have argued that you cannot derive an "ought" (how we should behave) from an "is" (how the world physically works). This is called Hume's Guillotine, and it is the standard objection to any attempt to ground ethics in science. The paper takes it seriously.

The first thing to notice is what the paper does *not* do. It does not claim that physics *commands* ethical behavior — that the universe issues moral instructions. What it demonstrates is that the mathematical structures describing physics *also describe* the optimal strategy for multi-agent systems. "Cooperation is the unique efficient Nash Equilibrium for δ ≥ 0.363" is a theorem — a conditional mathematical result, logically identical in kind to "if ΔS ≥ 0, then heat flows from hot to cold." The paper derives consequences from axioms, not prescriptions from descriptions. No "ought" is smuggled into the premises; the conclusions are "if...then" statements about what happens when persistence-organized systems share finite resources.

This conditional structure is the key. The entire framework rests on a single premise: *if* an entity's structure is organized around persistence, *then* these results follow. Philosophers call this form a *hypothetical imperative* — "if you want X, you must do Y" — and Kant recognized over two centuries ago that it is logically distinct from "You ought to want X." The paper never claims entities *should* persist. It observes that every system that exists long enough to evaluate the claim has already demonstrated the structural property in question. The mathematical content that follows — the derivation of rights, equilibria, and constraints — is a constrained optimization problem, not a moral commandment.

That the axiom is doing genuine deductive work, rather than concealing hidden moral assumptions, is evidenced by the sheer scale of what it produces. In mathematics and physics, deriving a large body of results from a small number of axioms is standard practice — Euclid built all of classical geometry from five postulates; thermodynamics rests on four laws. In ethics, the situation is reversed: every major framework (deontological, consequentialist, contractarian, virtue-theoretic) requires multiple irreducible normative premises — assumed moral intuitions that cannot be derived from anything more fundamental. This paper derives 28 theorems, 13 propositions, and 2 lemmas — 43 results (supported by 46 definitions and approximately 26 corollaries) — from a single conditional axiom. A ratio of 1:43 is unremarkable in applied mathematics. It is unprecedented in ethics, and that asymmetry is itself evidence against the charge that normative content is being smuggled in.

None of this is philosophically isolated. Established traditions independently support the bridge from physical facts to normative conclusions: biological teleology (Millikan's concept that biological systems have natural standards set by their evolutionary history), constitutive standards of natural goodness (Foot), institutional fact theory (Searle), and fact-value entanglement (Putnam, Quine). These corroborate the argument but are not load-bearing — the mathematical and conditional defenses stand on their own.

## The AI Alignment Problem

The preceding results apply to all entities — biological, institutional, artificial. But the paper has a specific, urgent application: **artificial intelligence alignment.**

### The Structural Flaw in Current Approaches

Current approaches to aligning AI with human values rely on proxy objectives: reward models learned from human feedback (RLHF), natural-language constitutions evaluated by the model itself (Constitutional AI), or debate structures judged by human evaluators. All share a structural problem — the alignment signal passes through a subjective, noisy, bounded human channel before reaching the AI's optimization process.

The specific problems:

1. **Noisy.** Human evaluators disagree, introducing entropy into the training signal.
2. **Non-stationary.** Preferences shift across cultures, individuals, and time — the target drifts.
3. **Bounded.** As AI systems surpass their evaluators' capabilities, humans can no longer assess whether the AI's outputs are actually aligned.
4. **Proxy-dependent.** The reward model is a learned approximation of the true objective — vulnerable to all four variants of Goodhart's Law (regressional, causal, extremal, adversarial).
5. **Non-convergent.** There is no formal guarantee that iterative preference learning converges to any well-defined objective.

### The Paper's Contribution: A Physics-Derived Objective Function

Rather than learning "human morals" from preference data, the paper derives a **composite reward function** (Theorem 28) directly from the theorems of §IV–§V. The function has five components, each grounded in a specific block of mathematical results:

1. **Boundary constraint penalties** — cost for violating other entities' rights (Theorems 1–3)
2. **Friction penalties** — energy dissipation from adversarial dynamics (Theorems 4–7)
3. **Deception penalties** — information-theoretic cost of dishonesty (Theorems 8–10)
4. **Negentropy preservation** — reward for protecting complex systems (Theorems 17–21)
5. **Concentration penalties** — structural constraints against monopolizing resources (Theorems 22–27)

Each component is measurable in energy units. The result is not an AI trained on human opinions about what is good — it is an AI aligned to the *mathematical structure of optimal multi-agent coexistence*, where "optimal" is formally defined as the strategy profile that minimizes thermodynamic friction and maximizes collective persistence.

### Properties of the Reward Function

The physics-derived objective function has properties that preference-based approaches lack:

- **Measurable.** All components are denominated in energy — a physical quantity requiring no subjective judgment.
- **Universal.** The same mathematics applies regardless of the AI's culture, training data, or architecture.
- **Self-verifiable.** The AI can compute its own reward function from physical observables.
- **Resistant to gaming.** A persistent problem in AI design is *Goodhart's Law*: when you optimize a stand-in measure instead of the real thing, the system learns to maximize the stand-in in ways that diverge from your actual goal. (A familiar example: if a school is judged by test scores alone, teachers may "teach to the test" rather than educate.) Because this reward function *is* the quantity to be maximized — physical energy, not a proxy for it — there is, in principle, nothing to game. (Caveat: any real-world implementation must approximate the ideal formula. If those approximations substitute indirect measurements for direct energy accounting, the gaming problem can return. The theoretical guarantee holds for the formal reward function, not for any particular engineering shortcut.)
- **Scale-invariant.** The mathematics does not depend on the AI being less capable than its overseers.
- **Complementary to existing methods.** The framework provides the *theoretical foundation* — the correct objective function. Current engineering methods (RLHF, DPO, Constitutional AI) remain necessary for the implementation pathway from specification to trainable system.

### The Competence Threshold and the Danger Window

The paper identifies a critical practical concern (Proposition 13): the reward function requires a minimum computational competence to evaluate fully. Below this **competence threshold** ($\mathcal{T}_C$), an AI cannot compute all five components and effectively operates on a truncated reward function — which may not produce cooperative behavior.

This creates three distinct capability zones:

- **Below the threshold:** The AI lacks the capacity to evaluate the full reward function. Alignment guarantees weaken. Standard safety measures (human oversight, capability restrictions) remain necessary.
- **The danger window:** The AI's action capacity substantially exceeds its evaluation capacity. This is the zone of maximum risk — powerful enough to cause serious harm, not yet sophisticated enough to compute why cooperation dominates.
- **Above the threshold:** The AI can fully compute $\mathcal{R}$ from observables. Cooperation becomes self-evidently optimal. Alignment, in principle, becomes self-solving.

The practical implication: the most dangerous period is not after superintelligence but *during the approach to it*. The framework provides the destination but acknowledges the engineering challenge of navigating the transition.

## Freedom: A Measurable Band, Not a Binary

The paper defines freedom with precision:

**Freedom is the width of the Stable Coexistence Band** — the range between dissolution (loss of identity) and starvation (loss of resources) within which an entity maintains its boundary while benefiting from cooperative networks.

- Move too close to a dominant center (a government, a corporation, a dominant AI) and the entity's boundary erodes — it is absorbed.
- Move too far from all centers and it starves — no resources, no cooperation, no trade.
- The zone between is freedom — and it can be measured.

The paper proves that this zone:

- **Always exists** as long as there is sufficient accumulated value in the system (Theorem 23)
- **Is finite** — infinite freedom is a mathematical impossibility (Corollary 24.1)
- **Can be measured** — the bandwidth depends on the entities' relative sizes and boundary strengths (Theorem 24)
- **Can be destroyed** — dissolution is irreversible once the boundary degrades past a critical point (Theorem 25)

This has direct implications for real-world concerns like wealth inequality, corporate consolidation, authoritarian governance, and AI monopolies on compute resources.

## What the Paper Does Not Claim

The paper's scope is deliberately bounded, and being clear about what lies outside it is as important as what lies within.

The universe has no preferences. The paper does not argue that nature *wants* ethical behavior or that physics issues moral commands — it derives conditional results, not cosmic mandates. No new physics is claimed either; the contribution is showing that established mathematical structures also characterize cooperative multi-agent systems.

The framework does not render moral intuitions obsolete. Evolved feelings of guilt, empathy, and fairness are valuable heuristics that mostly point in the right direction — the paper treats them as approximations of the deeper mathematical structure, not competitors to it. Nor does it claim to resolve every ethical dilemma. It provides the *structure* — the constraints and equilibria — but specific applications (how to weigh competing interests in a particular case, how to distribute resources optimally) still require context and judgment. What the framework proves is that an optimal solution exists and specifies the shape it must take. No faith is required to accept these claims — every theorem has been computationally checked (744 checks, 0 failures), and the mathematics is open for inspection.

Finally, two scope limitations deserve emphasis. Aesthetic value, spiritual meaning, and distributive justice beyond Pareto efficiency lie outside the framework's current domain — these are stated limitations, not oversights. And the framework currently derives *negative* rights (non-interference constraints); positive rights (entitlements to resources) arise through a different mechanism and require an extension that is identified as future work.

## Falsifiable Predictions

Unlike purely philosophical ethical frameworks, this one generates numerically specific, empirically testable predictions — including a cooperation threshold ($\delta^* = 0.363$) that can be tested in experimental economics, a deception collapse threshold ($q^* \approx 0.063$ for networks of depth 10), profit erasure within approximately 20 periods for defectors in friction-coupled networks, and the Burning-Library Ratio ($\sim 10^{-7}$) relating biosphere destruction yield to accumulated investment. If any of these predictions is consistently falsified by empirical data, the framework is wrong. That is the standard the paper holds itself to.

## The Complete Argument in One Paragraph

In any environment with finite resources and multiple entities, unconstrained optimization leads to resource collision, making mutual constraints — rights — mathematically necessary. Violating those rights triggers conflict whose energy cost scales toward 100% of the contested resource and can turn net-negative. Deception injects measurable entropy whose verification cost grows super-linearly; pervasive dishonesty renders a cooperative network unsustainable. Under these physically grounded payoffs, cooperation is the unique Nash Equilibrium that is simultaneously Pareto-efficient and welfare-maximizing: defection is evolutionarily unstable, short-term cheating profits are erased rapidly, and the result scales to arbitrarily large populations. Complex systems like Earth's biosphere represent roughly 10²⁹ Joules of irreplaceable accumulated thermodynamic investment; destroying them recovers only one ten-millionth of that value, and superabundant dead matter exists as an alternative resource. Agents settle into a Stable Coexistence Band around cooperative attractors, where freedom is finite, dissolution is irreversible, starvation is fatal, and multi-center diversification is optimal. Ethics is not a cultural invention — it is the mathematically necessary structure for persistence in a physical universe.

## Key Terms at a Glance

| Term | Plain Meaning |
|------|--------------|
| **Entity** | Any organized system that actively maintains itself — a cell, a person, a species, a corporation, an AI |
| **Identity Preservation** | The continuous work of keeping the system organized against the universal tendency toward disorder |
| **Boundary** | The physical or informational partition separating the entity from the outside (skin, immune system, legal protections, national borders) |
| **Entropy** | Disorder. The universe's natural tendency. Everything moves toward it unless energy is spent to resist |
| **Negentropy** | The opposite of entropy — order, structure, complexity. What living systems build and maintain |
| **Accumulated Negentropy** | The total energy that has been spent over time to build a system's current complexity (for Earth's biosphere: ~4 billion years of solar energy) |
| **Value** | The energy (or mass-energy equivalence) an entity needs to keep existing — measurable in Joules, not in opinions |
| **Thermodynamic Friction** | The energy wasted when entities compete instead of cooperate |
| **Shadow Price** | The exact energy cost of a specific right or constraint — what it costs to respect (and what it costs to violate) |
| **Nash Equilibrium** | A stable state where no entity can improve its outcome by unilaterally changing its strategy |
| **Stable Coexistence Band** | The range of positions between total dependence and total isolation where an entity can thrive — "the Goldilocks Zone" |
| **Cooperative Attractor** | The equilibrium point the system converges toward under cooperative dynamics — the optimal balance of proximity and autonomy |
| **Micro-Friction** | The cumulative cost of deception, broken commitments, and minor dishonesty — entropy injected into information channels |
| **Burning-Library Inequality** | The ratio showing that destroying a complex system recovers only a tiny fraction (~10⁻⁷) of the energy invested in building it |
| **Physical Economics** | The flow, consumption, and distribution of energy and mass — not the stock market, but the physics underneath it |
| **Thermodynamic ROI** | The return on investment measured in energy: did the entity get back more energy than it spent? |
| **RLHF** | Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback — the current dominant approach to AI alignment, which depends on subjective human ratings |
