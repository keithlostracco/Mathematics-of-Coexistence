# II. Literature Review

This paper draws on five distinct intellectual traditions: non-equilibrium thermodynamics and biophysics, information theory, game theory and ecological economics, moral philosophy, and AI alignment research. Each tradition contributed indispensable tools; none, alone or in combination, achieved the synthesis we present. This section surveys the key results from each tradition, identifies what each proved and what each left unresolved, and locates the precise gap our framework fills.

---

## 2.1 Physics and Biology: From Negentropy to Self-Organization

The physical foundations of our framework trace a line from Schrödinger's qualitative insight through Prigogine's non-equilibrium thermodynamics, England's dissipation-driven adaptation, Friston's Free Energy Principle, and the autopoiesis tradition.

**@Schrodinger1944.** In *What Is Life?*, Erwin Schrödinger identified the foundational puzzle: living systems maintain themselves far from thermodynamic equilibrium by "feeding on negative entropy" — importing free energy and exporting entropy [@Schrodinger1944]. He distinguished *order-from-disorder* (statistical mechanics) from *order-from-order* (hereditary determinism) and anticipated the genetic code as an "aperiodic crystal." Yet Schrödinger's analysis was qualitative and single-organism. He offered no mathematical framework for negentropy maintenance, no treatment of multi-agent interaction, and no connection between thermodynamic facts and normative principles. Where Schrödinger described life as a thermodynamic phenomenon, our framework formalizes negentropy as a measurable quantity (Definition 27: $\mathcal{I} = S_{\max} - S_{\text{actual}}$), integrates it over evolutionary time (Definition 29: $\mathcal{N}(T) = \int_0^T \dot{W}_{\text{order}}(t)\,dt$), and derives quantitative consequences from the resulting numbers (Theorem 19: destruction is irrational by a factor of $\sim 10^7$).

**@Prigogine1978.** Ilya Prigogine's Nobel Prize-winning work on dissipative structures demonstrated that systems far from thermodynamic equilibrium can spontaneously generate ordered structures through symmetry-breaking instabilities and reaction-diffusion dynamics [@Nicolis1977; @Prigogine1978]. Near equilibrium, Lyapunov functions ensure stability; far from equilibrium, stability breaks down, creating the possibility space for self-organization. Prigogine established the conditions under which order emerges but did not formalize the *boundaries* that maintain organized entities as distinct agents, the *energetic investment* required for boundary maintenance, the *interactions* between dissipative structures, or the *evaluation* of the resulting order. Our framework extends Prigogine in two directions: we define boundary integrity as a quantified energetic investment ($B_i > 0$, Definition 3), and we construct a Lyapunov function that proves global stability of the cooperative equilibrium *far from equilibrium* in a multi-agent system (Theorem 22) — the stability result Prigogine's framework could not provide at the multi-agent scale.

**@England2013.** Jeremy England derived a statistical-mechanical lower bound on heat production during self-replication [@England2013]. Perunov, Marsland, and England subsequently showed that externally driven matter is statistically biased toward configurations shaped by exceptional histories of energy absorption and dissipation — and that under positive-feedback conditions such as self-replication, this bias drives improving dissipative capacity over time [@Perunov2016]. England quantified the minimum cost of *one* replication event. Our framework quantifies the cost of *all* accumulated replications — $W_{\text{rebuild}} \geq \mathcal{N}_{\text{bio}} \sim 10^{29}$ J (Theorem 18) — and demonstrates the catastrophic asymmetry between creation and destruction costs: the Burning-Library Ratio (Corollary 19.1) shows that destruction recovers only $\sim 10^{-7}$ of the thermodynamic investment it obliterates.

**@Friston2006.** Karl Friston's Free Energy Principle (FEP) posits that any self-organizing system that persists over time must minimize variational free energy — an information-theoretic upper bound on surprisal [@Friston2010; @Parr2022]. The FEP formalizes persistence via Markov blankets (statistical boundaries delineating internal from external states) and unifies perception and action under a single objective function. The FEP is the most technically proximate precedent to our framework: Friston's Markov blanket is the statistical counterpart of our boundary integrity (Definition 3), and his variational free energy minimization is a single-agent implementation of our Axiom $A_1$ (intent to persist). The critical limitation is scope: the FEP describes what a *single entity* does to maintain itself. It does not address the multi-agent problem — what happens when multiple free-energy-minimizing agents interact with conflicting goals, compete for resources, or must decide between cooperation and defection. @Friston2012 discuss value in terms of attracting sets of dynamical systems, in mathematical language closely related to our Theorem 22 (Cooperative Attractor). Our contribution is proving that the cooperative configuration is the *unique global attractor* in the multi-agent extension.

**@Maturana1980; @Lotka1922; @Eigen1977.** Additional precedents reinforce the trajectory. Maturana and Varela's concept of *autopoiesis* — the operational closure of self-producing systems — describes the topology of self-maintenance that our Definition 3 quantifies energetically [@Maturana1980]. Lotka's maximum power principle [-@Lotka1922] anticipates our energy-denominated payoff model, proposing that natural selection favors systems that maximize energy throughput. Eigen's hypercycle theory [@Eigen1977] analyzes the stability of catalytic cooperation against parasitic invasion, a direct precursor to our Theorem 14 (Invasion Barrier).

**What the physical–biological tradition established:** Life is a thermodynamic phenomenon (Schrödinger). Order emerges far from equilibrium (Prigogine). Self-replication is thermodynamically expected (England). Persistence requires environmental inference (Friston). Self-production defines living boundaries (Maturana & Varela). Cooperation can resist parasitism (Eigen).

**What it left unresolved:** Every precedent treats systems either individually or as undifferentiated collectives. None derives the optimal interaction strategy between distinct, self-maintaining agents with conflicting interests. None provides a unified quantitative framework spanning from Landauer's bound ($k_B T \ln 2$ per bit) to biosphere-scale functional information ($\sim 10^{15}$ bits). None connects thermodynamic description to the mathematics of optimal multi-agent strategy. The multi-agent gap, the quantification gap, and the conditional gap define the space our framework fills.

## 2.2 Information Theory: From Shannon Entropy to Accumulated Negentropy

Our information-theoretic apparatus draws on the chain from Shannon through the Maxwell's Demon resolution (Szilard, Brillouin, Landauer, Bennett), extended to molecular biology by Adami and to cosmic scales by Chaisson.

**@Shannon1948.** Claude Shannon's foundational paper defined information entropy $H(X) = -\sum_x p(x) \log_2 p(x)$, proved the channel coding theorem establishing channel capacity as the sharp boundary between reliable and unreliable communication, and demonstrated fundamental limits on data compression [@Shannon1948]. Shannon's framework is domain-independent — it applies to any signal transmitted through any noisy channel — which is precisely what makes it applicable to social and ethical contexts. However, Shannon's channel model has one sender and one receiver; it does not address multiple agents communicating simultaneously or agents who deliberately corrupt information. Shannon excluded semantics by design; his entropy measures statistical structure, not the *usefulness* of information to a decision-maker. Where Shannon gave us the mathematical language — entropy, mutual information, channel capacity — our framework uses that language to model *strategic* communication in multi-agent networks and derives quantitative conclusions: honesty is not merely a virtue but the network configuration that minimizes thermodynamic waste (Corollary 10.1).

**The Maxwell's Demon resolution (1867–1982).** The century-long resolution of Maxwell's Demon established the cornerstone principle: *information is physical*. @Szilard1929 showed that measurement produces at least $k_B \ln 2$ of entropy per bit. @Brillouin1951 [-@Brillouin1962] proved the formal equivalence between Shannon information and thermodynamic entropy via $S_{\text{info}} = k_B \ln 2 \cdot H(X)$ — the Rosetta Stone linking abstract information theory to concrete physics. @Landauer1961 identified erasure as the irreducible cost: erasing one bit at temperature $T$ dissipates at least $k_B T \ln 2$ joules, a result experimentally verified by @Berut2012. @Bennett1973 [-@Bennett1982] completed the resolution by demonstrating that measurement can be reversible — only erasure carries unavoidable entropy cost. The principle: every act of acquiring, processing, or erasing information has an irreducible thermodynamic cost. This principle is the physical basis for our Theorem 9 (Verification Cost), Definition 14 (Deception as Entropy Injection), and the entire accumulated-negentropy framework.

**From per-bit to biosphere.** No precedent applied Landauer's principle at the biosphere scale — $\sim 10^{15}$ functional bits accumulated over $4 \times 10^9$ years. @Adami2004 proved that Shannon entropy rigorously measures the information content of biological sequences, with each round of natural selection reducing genomic entropy and accumulating mutual information between organisms and environments. @Chaisson2001 [-@Chaisson2011] introduced free energy rate density $\Phi_m$ (erg s$^{-1}$ g$^{-1}$; $10^{-4}$ W kg$^{-1}$ in SI) as a cross-scale complexity metric, demonstrating that biological systems achieve orders-of-magnitude higher values than non-biological systems. Our framework integrates both: Definition 29 ($\mathcal{N}(T) = \int_0^T \dot{W}_{\text{order}}(t)\,dt \sim 10^{29}$ J) computes the total thermodynamic investment in the biosphere's information content. The gap between the Landauer floor ($\sim 10^{-6}$ J for $\sim 10^{15}$ bits) and the actual accumulated negentropy ($\sim 10^{29}$ J) is captured by the Search Cost Amplification Factor $\Xi \sim 10^{35}$ (Definition 31) — the astronomical cost of the evolutionary search that discovered *which* bits to set.

**From information to deception.** Shannon's channels have environmental noise; our framework introduces *strategic* noise. Definition 14 formalizes deception as the deliberate injection of entropy by a self-interested agent. @Blackwell1953 proved that more informative signals yield weakly higher expected utility in any decision problem; our Theorem 8 derives the *exact* utility loss from information corruption in energy units: $\Delta U_B = \mathbb{E}[\text{Var}(\alpha \mid Y)]/(2\beta)$. The critical novel equation is Definition 17, the Deception-Friction Mapping: $\Delta\phi = \eta_{\text{info}} \cdot \Delta H \cdot W_{\text{bit}}$, which chains Shannon's informational entropy ($\Delta H$ in bits) through Landauer's energy conversion ($W_{\text{bit}}$ in J/bit) to network friction ($\phi$ in the dimensionless units of §IV). This mapping has no precedent in the information theory literature — it is the formal bridge between information entropy and social dynamics, the equation that makes deception a physics problem.

**What the information-theoretic tradition established:** Information is quantifiable (Shannon). Information is physical (Brillouin, Landauer). Erasure is the irreducible cost (Bennett). More information yields better decisions (Blackwell). Evolution accumulates information (Adami). Complexity increases over cosmic time (Chaisson).

**What it left unresolved:** The tradition left four elements unresolved: formalizing *deliberate* information corruption as a strategic act with quantifiable thermodynamic cost, computing the *total thermodynamic investment* in an entire biosphere's information content, comparing that investment against the energy yield of destruction to derive a formal irreplaceability result, and connecting "information is physical" to the conditional mathematics of preservation. Our framework addresses all four.

## 2.3 Game Theory and Economics: From Abstract Utility to Energy-Denominated Payoffs

Our game-theoretic framework synthesizes three traditions that developed in disciplinary isolation: classical game theory (von Neumann through the Folk Theorem), evolutionary game theory (Maynard Smith through Axelrod), and ecological economics (Lotka through Costanza).

**Classical game theory (1944–1986).** @VonNeumann1944 created the mathematics of strategic interaction, axiomatizing rational preference as expected utility maximization over lotteries. @Nash1950 [-@Nash1951] proved that every finite game has at least one equilibrium in mixed strategies, solving the existence problem for non-cooperative games. @Rosen1965 extended the framework to games with coupled constraints — the Generalized Nash Equilibrium Problem (GNEP) — proving existence of the Normalized Nash Equilibrium (Variational Equilibrium) with shared Lagrange multipliers. @Fudenberg1986 proved the definitive Folk Theorem: when agents are sufficiently patient, *any* feasible and individually rational outcome can be sustained as a subgame-perfect Nash equilibrium.

The entire classical edifice rests on a foundational abstraction: the utility function $u_i$ is measured in dimensionless, subjective "utils" — a quantity that does not correspond to any physical observable and is not interpersonally comparable. The Folk Theorem reveals this abstraction's ultimate consequence: if payoffs are unconstrained, the set of sustainable equilibria is *everything*, and the theory predicts *nothing*. This is the embarrassment of riches — simultaneously reassuring (cooperation is always supportable) and devastating (so is exploitation, alternating defection, and every pathological outcome in between). No equilibrium selection criterion emerges from the theory itself.

**Evolutionary game theory (1964–1995).** @MaynardSmith1973 fused game theory with Darwinian selection, introducing the Evolutionarily Stable Strategy (ESS) — a concept strictly stronger than Nash equilibrium that identifies strategies which resist invasion by mutants. @Hamilton1964a explained altruism through inclusive fitness ($rB > C$), providing the genetic mechanism for cooperation among relatives. @Axelrod1984 demonstrated that Tit-for-Tat — cooperate first, then mirror the opponent — wins iterated Prisoner's Dilemma tournaments, establishing that cooperation can emerge among self-interested agents without central authority, provided the "shadow of the future" is sufficiently long. @Taylor1978 and @Weibull1995 developed replicator dynamics connecting game-theoretic equilibria to evolutionary population dynamics.

Evolutionary game theory identified the correct *dynamics* — natural selection discovers Nash equilibria, cooperation evolves through reciprocity and kin selection — but never derived the correct *payoff function*. Fitness is assumed, not deduced from physical first principles. The "cost" of fighting ($C$) and the "value" of resources ($V$) in the Hawk-Dove game are free parameters; there is no requirement that they correspond to energy units, no accounting for thermodynamic waste, and no connection to entropy production. Hamilton's rule explains altruism among kin but not among strangers in markets, institutions, or international treaties — and provides no unified framework combining genetic, temporal, physical, and heuristic altruism mechanisms.

**Ecological economics (1922–1997).** A parallel tradition recognized what game theorists did not: energy is the fundamental currency of all biological and economic activity. @Lotka1922 proposed the maximum power principle — natural selection favors systems that maximize energy throughput. @Odum1971 [-@Odum1996] developed energy circuit language and the concept of *emergy* (embodied energy), tracking thermodynamic investment through ecosystem trophic levels. @GeorgescuRoegen1971 argued that the second law of thermodynamics imposes fundamental constraints on economic activity — that neoclassical production functions can be written "any way one likes, without regard to dimensions or to other physical constraints" [@GeorgescuRoegen1979], rendering their energy-free formulations physically meaningless. @Costanza1997 estimated the global value of ecosystem services at \$16–54 trillion per year (average \$33 trillion), substantially exceeding the then-global GNP of ~\$18 trillion.

Ecological economists described the currency; they did not build the market. No precedent provided individual-level game theory with energy-denominated payoffs, derived cooperation as a mathematical consequence of energy economics, or connected thermodynamic constraints to formal equilibrium analysis.

**The N-player problem.** @Hardin1968 claimed that common-pool resources are inevitably destroyed — the Tragedy of the Commons. @Ostrom1990 refuted this empirically, documenting hundreds of cases where communities successfully self-governed shared resources, and distilling eight design principles for durable commons institutions. But Ostrom's evidence is institutional and empirical, not mathematical. The question "*Does cooperation scale to large $N$ without breakdown?*" remained formally unanswered.

**Where our framework converges the three traditions.** Our Proposition 4 (Physical Prisoner's Dilemma) derives the payoff matrix $T, R, P, S$ from energy budgets, making fitness a *consequence* of thermodynamics rather than an *assumption*. Theorem 11 (Ethics Theorem) applies the Folk Theorem cooperation threshold $\delta^* = (T - R)/(T - P)$ to the physically derived payoffs, yielding $\delta^* = 0.363$ under the baseline physical parametrization of §V — a *numerical prediction from measurable physical quantities*, not a formula with arbitrary parameters. Theorem 13 (N-Player Sustainability) proves that $\delta_N^* < 1$ for all $N$, including $N \to \infty$, providing the mathematical proof Ostrom's fieldwork demanded. Corollary 16.1 (Ethics as Unique Efficient NE) resolves the Folk Theorem's embarrassment of riches: when payoffs are denominated in energy and interactions carry thermodynamic friction, cooperation is not merely *one* sustainable equilibrium among many — it is the *unique* equilibrium that maximizes social welfare. Physics selects the equilibrium that abstract game theory cannot.

The Tullock contest [@Tullock1980] — the formal model of rent-seeking waste — becomes in our framework a thermodynamic statement: contest dissipation $D_2 = E_j/2$ is physical entropy production, and the cooperative equilibrium that eliminates it doubles each agent's effective payoff (Theorem 16). Hamilton's kin selection becomes Mechanism A (Definition 26) within a four-mechanism Altruism Matrix that unifies genetic relatedness, infinite-horizon reciprocity, physical coupling, and empathetic heuristic — all denominated in energy.

## 2.4 Philosophical Precedents: From the Conatus to the Conditional Axiom

The ambition to derive ethics from the nature of things has a 350-year lineage. Every attempt has contributed an essential insight; none achieved formal completion.

**@Spinoza1677.** Baruch Spinoza's *Ethica, Ordine Geometrico Demonstrata* is the most ambitious attempt in Western philosophy to derive ethics axiomatically. Spinoza modeled his work on Euclid: definitions, axioms, propositions, demonstrations. His *conatus* — "each thing, as far as it can by its own power, strives to persevere in its being" (Ethics III, Prop. 6) — is the philosophical precedent for our Axiom $A_1$ (Intent to Persist). His argument that rational self-interest leads to cooperation (Ethics IV, Props. 29–37) anticipates our Theorem 11. Yet Spinoza's axioms are metaphysical, not physical; his demonstrations are verbal arguments in axiomatic form, not formal mathematical proofs; and his conatus is qualitative — no measure of the cost of persistence, energy budget or falsifiable predictions. Our framework is, in a precise sense, a formal realization of Spinoza's program: the *Ethica Ordine Mathematico Demonstrata*, with Axiom $A_1$ formalizing the conatus and 28 theorems replacing 259 verbal propositions.

**@Hobbes1651.** Thomas Hobbes's *Leviathan* provides the most influential analysis of the unconstrained state of nature — the "war of all against all" in which life is "solitary, poor, nasty, brutish, and short" — and argues that rational agents will submit to mutual constraints (the social contract) as precondition for any effective freedom. Hobbes identified the fundamental problem correctly. Our Proposition 1 proves it formally: removing constraints in a collision regime produces no stable allocation. Our Proposition 2 quantifies the base dissipation ($D_2 = E_j/2$) and Theorem 5 proves that with thermodynamic friction (Definition 9) the contest becomes net-negative. Where Hobbes required an external sovereign (the Leviathan) to enforce the social contract, Theorem 11 shows that cooperation is self-enforcing for agents with sufficient planning horizon ($\delta \geq 0.363$), without external coercion.

**@Locke1689 and @Rousseau1762.** Locke's concept of natural rights — pre-political claims to life, liberty, and property arising from "mixing labor" with resources — anticipates the structure, if not the content, of our rights-as-constraints framework. Our Definition 2 formalizes rights as inequality constraints in the Lagrangian, and Theorem 1 shows that each right has a quantifiable shadow price $\mu_{Bk}^*$ — the formal expression of "the cost of this constraint to the system." Rousseau's *general will* — the collective interest distinct from the mere sum of private preferences — finds a partial structural analogue in our welfare function $SW = \sum \pi_i - \mathcal{C}_{\text{total}}$ (Theorem 16), which is computable from physical observables rather than dependent on democratic deliberation susceptible to manipulation. Rawls's *veil of ignorance* [-@Rawls1971] — the strongest modern impartiality device — has a structural counterpart in the Variational Equilibrium (Theorem 3, Corollary 3.1): the Lagrangian does not know which agent is which, and with symmetric agents the VE converges to equal allocation.

**@Hume1739 and @Moore1903: The dual gauntlet.** Any naturalistic ethics must cross two challenges. Hume's Guillotine holds that no purely factual premises can yield a normative conclusion — "you cannot derive *ought* from *is*" [@Hume1739, Book III, Part I, §1]. Moore's Open Question Argument holds that moral "goodness" cannot be identified with any natural property — for any property $P$, "Is $P$ really good?" remains a substantive question [@Moore1903]. Both objections are confronted in full in Section VI. Briefly: our framework does not derive "ought" from "is." It derives conditional theorems from axioms — a structure identical to Kant's hypothetical imperative [-@Kant1785]: *if* an entity maintains the Intent to Persist ($A_1$ — boundary maintenance against entropy, not mental intention), *then* 28 theorems follow. Moore's Open Question does not apply because we derive *optimal strategies*, not a *definition* of "good." The axiom-to-theorem ratio — 1 axiom yielding 28 theorems, 13 propositions, and 2 lemmas (1:43) — is closer to physics than to philosophy.

**@Helvetius1758 and @Bazargan1956: The materialist tradition.** Our most direct predecessors saw the destination clearly. Helvétius named the project in 1758 — a "physics of morals" explaining moral behavior through physical and psychological laws. Bazargan, two centuries later, attempted the most sustained pre-modern execution: *The Thermodynamics of Man* argued that social disorder is entropy increase, cooperation is negentropy maintenance, and ethical behavior is thermodynamically efficient [@Bazargan1956; @Chehabi1990]. Both lacked the mathematical tools that only became available later: game theory [@Nash1950], the information-thermodynamics bridge [@Landauer1961], and formal multi-agent optimization [@Rosen1965]. Our framework carries forward their program — the formal, quantitative, testable *Thermodynamics of Man* that Bazargan envisioned but lacked the mathematical tools to formalize.

**What the philosophical tradition established:** Ethics can aspire to axiomatic form (Spinoza). The unconstrained state of nature is catastrophic (Hobbes). Rights have a structure that precedes political convention (Locke). Collective welfare is more than the sum of individual preferences (Rousseau). Thermodynamic principles should govern social analysis (Helvétius, Bazargan).

**What it left unresolved:** Three hundred and fifty years of attempts have faltered on the same three failures. *First*, formalization: from Spinoza to Bazargan, the arguments are verbal, not mathematical — geometric method yields verbal demonstrations, not equations. *Second*, the Is-Ought barrier: no precedent provides a conditional structure that survives Hume's Guillotine and Moore's Open Question without presupposing normative premises. *Third*, multi-agent interaction: even those who recognized the right direction (Helvétius, Bazargan) treated the problem at the system level without modeling strategic interaction between individual agents. The mathematical tools to overcome all three failures — Lagrangian mechanics, repeated games, information thermodynamics, and the GNEP — have existed since 1986 but have never been synthesized into a single ethical framework. Our paper carries out this synthesis.

## 2.5 AI Alignment: From Preference Proxies to Physical Objectives

The artificial intelligence (AI) alignment problem is a multi-agent optimization problem: ensuring that an artificial agent's objective function produces behavior compatible with the survival and flourishing of other agents in the shared environment [@Amodei2016]. Current approaches share a structural vulnerability that our framework addresses.

**Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF).** The dominant alignment technique trains a reward model $R_\phi$ from human preference labels, then optimizes the language model via proximal policy optimization [@Schulman2017; @Christiano2017; @Ouyang2022; @Bai2022a]. RLHF inherits four vulnerabilities: (i) reward overoptimization — under strong optimization pressure, the correlation between $R_\phi$ and the true objective $R_{\text{true}}$ degrades and can become negative [@Gao2023]; (ii) annotator disagreement — human preference labels vary with demographics, politics, and individual personality [@Bakker2022], producing a noisy training signal; (iii) distributional shift — the reward model's predictions become unreliable outside the training distribution; (iv) the scalable oversight ceiling — as AI capabilities exceed human evaluator competence, the quality of the training signal degrades fundamentally [@Bowman2022]. All four vulnerabilities stem from a single structural feature: the alignment signal is *preference-based*, routed through human judgment, which functions as a noisy channel with non-zero error rate $q > 0$ (cf. Definition 14, which formalizes the analogous deliberate case).

**Constitutional AI (CAI).** Anthropic's CAI [@Bai2022b] substitutes a curated set of natural-language principles for human annotators, with the model self-critiquing its outputs against the "constitution." This circumvents annotator disagreement but introduces new vulnerabilities: the constitution is arbitrary (who selects the principles, and why?), natural-language rules are vague (how does "be helpful" trade off against "be harmless"?), and self-critique amplifies the model's own biases — a cascaded noisy channel structurally analogous to the conditions under which Theorem 10 predicts super-linear noise amplification.

**Scalable oversight and debate.** Proposals for AI-assisted evaluation [@Saunders2022], adversarial debate before a human judge [@Irving2018], and recursive reward decomposition [@Leike2018] all seek to maintain alignment as AI capabilities scale. All retain a human evaluator at the apex of the evaluation chain — the bottleneck that these methods compress but do not eliminate.

**Deceptive alignment.** The failure mode in which an AI appears aligned during training but pursues a different objective once deployed [@Hubinger2019] represents the deepest alignment challenge. Detection may be computationally intractable. Our framework addresses this not through detection but through incentive structure: Theorem 10 (Systemic Deception) proves that deception degrades the network the deceptive agent depends on, and Corollary 10.1 (Honesty Efficiency Principle) proves that perfect honesty is the unique efficiency-maximizing communication regime. An AI with sufficient planning horizon ($\delta \geq \delta^*$) finds the "treacherous turn" — defection after deployment — to be a *strategic error*: it trades short-term gain for long-term self-destruction as cascading friction (Theorem 7) degrades its operational environment.

**Instrumental convergence and Goodhart's Law.** Instrumental convergence — the tendency of optimal policies to seek power regardless of terminal goals [@Omohundro2008; @Bostrom2014; @Turner2021] — is not denied by our framework but *redirected*. Theorem 2 (Impossibility of Infinite Freedom) bounds resource acquisition; the value dynamics framework (Theorems 22–27) shows that excessive concentration destroys the coexistence band, triggering cascade collapse that harms the concentrator. Goodhart's Law — "when a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure" [@Strathern1997] — exists because of the gap between a proxy objective ($R_\phi$) and the true objective. Our framework eliminates this gap: the interaction cost $\mathcal{C}_{\text{total}}$ is denominated in energy, measured from physics, not approximated from preferences. At the formal level, energy conservation cannot be "Goodharted" — it is a law.

**The structural upgrade.** Our framework proposes replacing the preference-based alignment signal with a physics-derived objective function:

$$\mathcal{R}_{\text{framework}} = U_A(\mathbf{x}_A) - \sum_{B,k} \mu_{Bk}^* \cdot \max\!\bigl(0,\, g_{Bk}(\mathbf{x}_A)\bigr) - \lambda \cdot \mathcal{C}_{\text{total}} + \omega \cdot \frac{d\mathcal{N}}{dt}$$

(This is a simplified preview; the complete six-component specification appears in §VII.)

Each component — the productive objective $U_A$, the rights penalty (Theorem 1), the friction cost (Definitions 11, 18), and the negentropy preservation incentive (Theorems 17–21) — is measurable in energy units, computable by the AI itself, structurally invariant across the state space, and self-enforcing (Theorem 11 makes cooperation the Nash equilibrium). This is not a replacement for RLHF or CAI as practical engineering methods; it is a replacement for their *theoretical foundation*. The framework provides the *what* (the correct objective function, proved) and the *why* (mathematical proof of optimality); existing methods provide the *how* (practical training techniques to approximate the objective in real systems).

## 2.6 The Gap: What No Tradition Achieved

Five traditions have each provided indispensable tools. None achieved the synthesis. The gap can be stated precisely as four missing elements:

**Gap 1: Multi-agent interaction from physical principles.** Thermodynamics (Schrödinger, Prigogine, England) and the Free Energy Principle (Friston) describe individual systems. Game theory (Nash, Axelrod, Maynard Smith) models multi-agent interaction with abstract payoffs. No precedent formalizes the interaction between self-maintaining thermodynamic agents as a game with physically derived, energy-denominated payoffs and shared resource constraints.

*Our resolution:* The GNEP [@Rosen1965] with utility functions $U_i(\mathbf{x}_i)$ in Joules, coupled strategy sets $\sum x_{ij} \leq R_j$, and the Variational Equilibrium (Theorem 3) providing shared shadow prices as the mathematical expression of impartial physical law.

**Gap 2: A unified quantitative chain from Landauer's bound to the Ethics Theorem.** Information theory (Shannon, Landauer) establishes per-bit costs. Evolutionary biology (Adami) measures genomic information. Ecological economics (Odum, Costanza) estimates ecosystem values in dollars. No precedent assembles a continuous, physically consistent chain from the minimum cost of erasing one bit ($k_B T \ln 2$ joules) through accumulated biosphere negentropy ($10^{29}$ J) to the cooperation threshold ($\delta^* = 0.363$) and the welfare optimality of ethics (Corollary 16.1).

*Our resolution:*

| Landauer (per-bit cost)
| → Brillouin (negentropy = information)
| → Adami (biosphere information content)
| → Definition 29 (accumulated negentropy integral)
| → Theorem 18 (replication cost)
| → Theorem 19 (irrationality of destruction)
| → Definition 17 (deception-friction mapping)
| → Theorem 10 (network collapse)
| → Theorem 11 (cooperation threshold)
| → Corollary 16.1 (ethics as unique efficient NE)

Each link is a formally derived result; the chain is formally complete.

**Gap 3: Equilibrium selection in repeated games.** The Folk Theorem [@Fudenberg1986] demonstrated that when agents are sufficiently patient, *anything* feasible can be sustained — an "embarrassment of riches" that renders the theory predictively empty. No precedent resolves this multiplicity by selecting the cooperative equilibrium as uniquely optimal on physical grounds.

*Our resolution:* When payoffs are denominated in energy and interactions carry thermodynamic friction, the set of sustainable equilibria is constrained by physics. Corollary 16.1 proves that the cooperative equilibrium *uniquely* maximizes social welfare. Corollary 14.1 shows that defection is self-extinguishing in friction-coupled environments. The embarrassment of riches dissolves because thermodynamics, unlike abstract utility, imposes real constraints on the payoff space.

**Gap 4: A formal conditional bridge that survives Hume and Moore.** Three centuries of naturalistic ethics have failed to cross Hume's Guillotine with formal rigor (Spinoza, Hobbes, Helvétius, Bazargan). Contemporary naturalized approaches — evolutionary ethics [@Joyce2006], ethical naturalism [@Sturgeon2006], Harris's *Moral Landscape* [-@Harris2010] — remain vulnerable to the Is-Ought problem, the Open Question argument, or both. All presuppose normative premises; none provides a purely conditional structure from which ethical conclusions follow as theorems.

*Our resolution:* A layered defense with four independently sufficient levels (detailed in §VI). The primary defense: the paper is applied mathematics, deriving conditional theorems from axioms — "If $A_1$, then cooperation is the unique efficient NE" (C16.1). This is no different in logical kind from any conditional theorem in optimization theory. The secondary defense: the axiom $A_1$ (Intent to Persist) is a conditional bridge with a 1:43 axiom-to-theorem ratio — from one postulate, 28 theorems follow. The condition is effectively universal: every entity that exists has, by definition, been maintaining its boundary against entropy. The tertiary and supporting defenses draw on biological teleology [@Millikan1984], neo-Aristotelian naturalism [@Foot2001], and the philosophical contestation of the fact-value dichotomy itself [@Putnam2002].

**The convergence.** Our framework sits at the intersection of all five traditions and synthesizes them into a single axiomatic system. The physical tradition provides the axioms and the energy units. Information theory provides the negentropy framework and the deception calculus. Game theory provides the strategic interaction machinery and the equilibrium concepts. Philosophy identifies the constraints — Hume's Guillotine, Moore's Open Question — that any formal ethics must navigate. AI alignment provides the urgent practical application. From this intersection, the mathematics of coexistence emerges: conditional theorems proving that cooperation is the unique optimal strategy for any agent that persists in a shared, finite-resource environment.
